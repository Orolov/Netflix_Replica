About The Abyss of Racism

At the tail end of 1942, the Empire of Japan surprised America by attacking their naval base at Pearl harbor. America responded by declaring war on the Empire of Japan. Because of the large population of Asians, mostly Japanese, on the west coast of America many were afraid that the Japanese population were going to sabotage the war effort and endanger America. The president did not want to take any risks, so America began to incarcerate Japanese citizens and imprison them in internment camps.

Fred Korematsu heard about the news and did not budge from his home. He believed as an American citizen he should be treated fairly and not be imprisoned. He was arrested instead of sent to a camp. After his arrest for failing to report to a relocation center, Korematsu while in jail joined the American Civil Liberties Union. He joined the ACLU to challenge the constitutionality of the government’s order to relocate all Japanese citizens.

When the trial began, Korematsu was tried in San Francisco in a federal court, for violating military orders issued under Executive Order. Korematsu had been convicted for his crimes and was given five years of probation and was sent to an Assembly Center in San Bruno. Korematsu had tried to appeal the trial but he was unsuccessful. Korematsu continued to ask the Supreme Court to retrial. The Supreme Court had tried him and had a split 6-3 decision determining Korematsu’s verdict was a “military necessity” and not about race. The decision on Korematsu’s conviction was highly controversial as it was the one of few supreme court cases that sustained a restriction on civil liberties, seeming to approve racism. However, the conviction was universally rejected, but finally overturned during the 2018 Trump v. Hawaii Supreme Court case where Chief Justice Roberts declared that Korematsu’s case was unlawfully decided.
